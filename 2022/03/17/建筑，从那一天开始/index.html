建筑，从那一天开始

Created2022-03-17|Updated2022-12-15|Reading and writing
|Post View:

Toyo Ito writes in “Architecture After That Day,” “I cannot help but think about what I have been designing buildings for.” “And for whom and for what are they designed?” He recalls the leisure and relaxation of his birth in Korea and his upbringing in the countryside of Shinshu until “Tokyo” struck him; its modernity and futuristic feel became the dream world he pursued. For the next fifty years, he worked hard in Tokyo to build his architectural dream.
However, gradually doubts emerge, and he asks himself if these huge cities have become the result of the visualization of huge capital. If the absolute dream and the support of romance have been lost. … Just as these doubts were taking shape, the 311 earthquakes struck. When he went to the disaster area, he said, “Maybe I, who was always going toward Tokyo, returned to the natural place after a detour in that journey.”
What is the “natural place” to which he returned? I think Ito is talking about the return of humanity. Especially when you talk about it as an architect, there should be a sense of ethics and goodwill toward a society in this role. In the past, the beauty of the architect’s profession often came from the beauty of the “object” (building). Still, the true beauty of architecture should come from the purpose of the architectural activity. Ito says that the natural beauty of architecture should come from the architect’s concern for people.
Architecture as an industry implies a conflicting duality: on the one hand, it often serves the capital, but on the other hand, it can be developed to serve society and people. Even if architecture is purely a private industry and investment, it necessarily uses land and depletes resources while employing people and providing economic statistics. The act of building has been made a part of “society.”
When the act of architecture is crossed over to the concept of “social design,” the nature of the act of architecture implied is often directly linked to design activities such as charity, disaster relief, etc. Although this connection is close to actual needs, a broader definition comes from Victor Papanek’s statement in 1971 that social design is what is designed for “real needs”: design thinking to solve problems that people, society, or cities must face. Design thinking is used to solve the problems that people, society, or cities must face and to make a real impact. Such “design” works are called social design.
On the one hand, it criticizes the architectural resources for being too skewed towards “unreality” and distorting this unreality into the illusion of reality. On the other hand, it gives the design industry a genuine demand for reality and can transform this design into an influential social factor. In architectural design, the familiar public housing is one of the key elements; another project can be broadly described as the educational process of design as diffusion of ideas and the development of impact.
